01 · Problem

One Plan.
Millions of People.

Jefit's 13M+ users were handed the same static workout plans — regardless of fitness level, equipment access, schedule, or goals. The result: high drop-off within the first two weeks, and a product that felt built for an imaginary average person.

The core tension: personalization at scale without overwhelming the user. How do you make every user feel like Jefit was built for them, while keeping onboarding frictionless?

Problem Statement
“How might we replace generic, static plans with an adaptive experience that responds to each user's real-world fitness context — without adding cognitive load?”

02 · Research

Understanding
the Real User

I led a mixed-methods research sprint over 3 weeks: in-depth interviews with 12 active users, analysis of Amplitude drop-off funnels, and a competitive audit across personalized fitness apps. Three core failure modes emerged.

😤
Plans feel irrelevant fast
Within 2 weeks a plan no longer reflected actual schedule or energy levels, leading to abandonment.
🏋
Equipment mismatch
34% of users traveled frequently or worked out at home, but plans assumed full gym access every session.
📈
No sense of progression
Static plans don't acknowledge improvement — removing a key motivational trigger for continued use.
🤯
Configuration anxiety
When asked to “customize” a plan, 61% of users felt overwhelmed and defaulted to a preset — then abandoned it within a week.
“I keep restarting the same plan because I feel like I'm failing it — not that it's wrong for me.”
— Interview participant, 28, intermediate lifter

03 · Ideation

From Insight
to Direction

I ran a cross-functional workshop with product and engineering teams using HMW → Crazy 8s → Dot Vote. Three viable directions emerged, scored against user need, engineering feasibility, and business value.

🤖
AI-Adaptive Engine — Chosen
Fully automated plan adjustment based on workout history, recovery signals, and schedule inputs. High impact, high complexity.
🎭
Manual Customization+
Enhanced editing tools to swap exercises or adjust frequency. Lower lift, but doesn't solve the core “I don't know what to change” problem.
💬
Coach-Guided Prompts
Weekly check-in moments where users report how the week went, and the plan adjusts accordingly. Hybrid AI + human voice.
Decision
Direction 1 as north star, with direction 3's “check-in moment” as the primary user-facing interaction. The AI adapts in the background; the UX feels like a coaching conversation — not a settings page.

05 · Prototype & Test

5 Rounds of
Usability Testing

I ran 5 rounds of moderated usability testing across 3 months, iterating after each round. Sessions included 4–6 participants representing beginner, intermediate, and advanced fitness levels.

🔄
Rounds 1–2: Trust
Users didn't trust AI-generated changes without explanation. Added plain-language rationale cards — “here's why we changed this.”
🎯
Round 3: Control
Users wanted an easy override option. Added “Edit anyway” below every AI suggestion without making it the primary action.
⏱
Round 4: Timing
The weekly check-in at Sunday night outperformed Monday morning by 2.3x — users were more reflective at week's end.
🏆
Round 5: Delight
Added a subtle progress visualization showing adaptation history. Users described the plan as feeling “alive.”

07 · Reflection

What I Learned

Trust is the product. The AI engine was sophisticated, but the design challenge was entirely about making users feel in control of it. The plain-language rationale was the highest-impact design decision — not the algorithm.

Timing is a design decision. Discovering that Sunday outperformed Monday for check-ins reminded me that when you interrupt a user matters as much as how.

0→1 requires protecting the vision. Stakeholders pushed to simplify the check-in to a single tap. I advocated for the conversational model based on research, and the retention data validated that choice.
