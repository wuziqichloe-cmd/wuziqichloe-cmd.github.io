02 · Problem

Too Generic to Keep Anyone

Jefit's Paid users were leaving before they ever found value — and the drop-off happened at two stages. Most abandoned their plan during onboarding, never reaching the activation point. Those who did stick around soon realized the plan wasn't keeping up with them — too static, too generic, and never tailored to their progress or preferences. Either way, it was easy to walk away.

The core tension: personalization at scale without overwhelming the user. How do you make every user feel like Jefit was built for them, while keeping onboarding frictionless?

Problem Statement
“How might we replace generic, static plans with an adaptive experience that responds to each user's real fitness context — so every user feels confident, trusts the algorithm, and can get started without a steep learning curve?”
03 · Research

Understanding
the Real User

I led a mixed-methods research sprint over 3 weeks: in-depth interviews with 12 active users, analysis of Amplitude drop-off funnels, and a competitive audit across personalized fitness apps. Three core failure modes emerged.

😤
Plans feel irrelevant fast
Within 2 weeks a plan no longer reflected actual schedule or energy levels, leading to abandonment.
🏋
Equipment mismatch
34% of users traveled frequently or worked out at home, but plans assumed full gym access every session.
📈
No sense of progression
Static plans don't acknowledge improvement — removing a key motivational trigger for continued use.
🤯
Configuration anxiety
When asked to “customize” a plan, 61% of users felt overwhelmed and defaulted to a preset — then abandoned it within a week.
“I keep restarting the same plan because I feel like I'm failing it — not that it's wrong for me.”
— Interview participant, 28, intermediate lifter
04 · Ideation

From Insight
to Direction

I ran a cross-functional workshop with product and engineering teams using HMW → Crazy 8s → Dot Vote. Three viable directions emerged, scored against user need, engineering feasibility, and business value.

🤖
AI-Adaptive Engine — Chosen
Fully automated plan adjustment based on workout history, recovery signals, and schedule inputs. High impact, high complexity.
🎭
Manual Customization+
Enhanced editing tools to swap exercises or adjust frequency. Lower lift, but doesn't solve the core “I don't know what to change” problem.
💬
Coach-Guided Prompts
Weekly check-in moments where users report how the week went, and the plan adjusts accordingly. Hybrid AI + human voice.
Decision
Direction 1 as north star, with direction 3's “check-in moment” as the primary user-facing interaction. The AI adapts in the background; the UX feels like a coaching conversation — not a settings page.
05 · Strategy

Positioning for
Impact

With the AI-Adaptive Engine direction chosen, I partnered with product and growth to shape the business strategy. Three principles guided the plan: freemium entry with a clear value moment before any paywall, habit-first onboarding that lets users experience adaptation before being asked to pay, and gradual personalization that deepens as the AI learns each user.

The key strategic insight: users needed to feel the plan adapting to them before the paywall appeared. Based on our retention data, we aligned the conversion moment to the second adaptation cycle — when perceived value peaked at days 10–14.

🎯
Freemium Model
Core plan generation free. Advanced personalization, recovery-based adjustments, and priority modes behind a single paywall tier — simple, clear upgrade path.
📈
Habit-First Flow
Paywall delayed until week 2, after users experience at least one adaptation cycle. Data showed peak value perception at day 10–14, not day 1.
🔄
Feedback Flywheel
Every completed workout feeds the engine: better data → better adaptations → higher retention → more data. A self-reinforcing loop.
🚀
Phased Rollout
Launched to 5% of new users, iterated on conversion data for two weeks, then scaled to 100% — de-risking paywall timing and adaptation quality.
06 · Design Process

Designing the
Experience

01
Information Architecture
Mapped the full plan ecosystem. Identified three critical moments to inject adaptation: plan start, mid-week check-in, and post-workout feedback.
02
Wireframes & Flow
Three distinct user flows: onboarding without form-fatigue, weekly adaptation with clear rationale, and manual override when the AI's suggestion doesn't fit.
03
Visual Design
High-fidelity screens across iOS and Android. The adaptation notification used a distinct “coach card” pattern — warm, conversational, not algorithmic. Every change explained in plain language.

08 · Reflection

What I Learned

Trust is the product. The AI engine was sophisticated, but the design challenge was entirely about making users feel in control of it. The plain-language rationale was the highest-impact design decision — not the algorithm.

Timing is a design decision. Discovering that Sunday outperformed Monday for check-ins reminded me that when you interrupt a user matters as much as how.

0→1 requires protecting the vision. Stakeholders pushed to simplify the check-in to a single tap. I advocated for the conversational model based on research, and the retention data validated that choice.
