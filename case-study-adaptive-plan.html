02 · Problem

Too Generic to Keep Anyone

The drop-off pattern was hard to miss.

Paid users were handed a static 28-day plan — same exercises every week, no matter their goals or progress. What they paid for wasn’t growing with them.

The drop-off happened in two waves. Most never made it through activation. Those who did soon realized the plan couldn’t keep up — too generic to feel personal, too static to reflect their progress. Either way, walking away was the easier choice.

What we’d already tried

Tooltips, walkthroughs, video demos, even a UI refresh — four onboarding investments shipped. Metrics barely moved, and the reviews kept naming the same culprit.

The fix wasn’t the introduction. It was the plan itself.

03 · Research

Understanding
the Real User

I had two halves of the picture — when users left, and what they said. The missing piece was why.

So I spent three weeks listening at scale. First, the quantitative: Amplitude mapped drop-off by cohort and day. Those patterns — read through my lens as both designer and user of the app — became the hypotheses to test. Then the qualitative: with 12M users, the surface was too wide to read manually, so AI clustered themes across NPS, reviews, community, beta feedback, and Zendesk. 10 in-depth interviews validated and expanded them. The funnel told me where users dropped; the voices told me why.

A · What the funnel said
Day 1 remains the single biggest drop.

This pattern holds across every other pre-built plan we benchmarked — activation remains the universal bottleneck.

The longer users stay, the more they keep staying.

Retention curves flatten sharply after week 1. Users who clear the early hurdle compound — so the work was clear: get them past it.

B · What the voices said
No sense of progression
Static plans don’t acknowledge improvement — removing a key motivational trigger for continued use.
Routine override
For lifters with an existing routine, the app forced every plan to start from scratch — overriding the training habits they’d already built.
Injury blind spot
Half of interviewees had trained through an injury — but the plan didn’t adapt, and they didn’t know how to adjust.
Equipment mismatch
Users train across work, home, and commercial gyms — and even within the same category, available machines vary. But plans assumed identical equipment every session.
Lack of customization and direction
The 28-day plan ran straight with no rest days and a fixed exercise count per day — punishing for beginners and impossible to scale down.

How might we create an adaptive plan that feels personal & trustworthy — without overwhelming the user?

04 · Design Solution

From a 28-day fixed plan
to a living system

Every feature traces back to a research pain point — seven in the core product, two in monetization and retention.

Solves · Routine override

Training preference.

Users set their preferred split, exercise types, and pacing up front. The plan builds around those habits instead of overwriting them.

▶ Training preference
Solves · Injury blind spot

Injury management.

Log an injury or area of pain — affected exercises swap to safe substitutes. A check-in every 14 days updates recovery progress, guiding the user back to full training.

▶ Injury management
Solves · Equipment mismatch

Equipment management.

Every session adapts to whatever’s available that day: full gym, home setup, travel, or bodyweight-only — no manual reshuffling.

▶ Equipment management
Solves · No sense of progression

Load progression.

Weight, reps, and rest scale automatically from feedback and historical performance. Progression is visible, with deload weeks respected.

▶ Load progression
Solves · Lack of customization

Feedback module.

After each session with incomplete exercises, users can provide input — and those signals feed back into the engine to recalibrate intensity and recovery for the next session.

▶ Feedback module
Solves · Plans feel irrelevant fast

Mesocycle plan that adapts each week.

The plan rebalances volume, intensity, and recovery week over week — based on what the user actually did, not what was prescribed on day one.

▶ Mesocycle adaptation
Solves · Learning-curve overload

Familiarity module.

In dogfooding I noticed the generator recommending completely different exercises within the same muscle group week over week — a steep learning curve that pushed users to modify or abandon their plans. I raised it with PM and backend, and we retuned the generator to anchor in each user’s proven exercise history and introduce a small percentage of new movements gradually, week by week — familiarity without the learning cliff.

▶ Familiarity module
Monetization & Engagement
Monetization

Paywall — at the moment of value.

The premium paywall surfaces when users hit the ceiling of the free plan’s static behavior — framing adaptation as the value, not the gate.

▶ Paywall
Retention

Nudges.

Two jobs: build confidence for users who haven’t opted in yet, and keep the adaptive plan visible after the first GTM popup is dismissed — resurfacing it at the right moment instead of relying on a single entry point.

▶ Nudges
05 · Behind the Scenes

How It Came
Together

The shipped experience hides a long tail of decisions — flows that were thrown out, an IA that was redrawn twice, business and engineering constraints that reshaped the brief. Open any chapter below to see the work underneath.

01 User Flow

Three flows carried the experience: onboarding without form-fatigue, weekly adaptation with a clear rationale, and a manual override when the AI’s suggestion didn’t fit. The hardest call was the override path — giving users escape hatches without undermining trust in the engine.

02 Information Architecture

I mapped the full plan ecosystem to find the three moments where adaptation actually changes user behavior: plan start, mid-week check-in, and post-workout feedback. Anywhere else, an “adaptation” would feel like noise.

03 Strategy — business & tech constraints

With the direction set — build a plan that adapts, not another onboarding — I partnered with product and growth on positioning. Three principles guided the plan: freemium entry with a clear value moment before any paywall, habit-first onboarding that lets users experience adaptation before being asked to pay, and gradual personalization that deepens as the AI learns each user.

The strategic insight: users needed to feel the plan adapting to them before the paywall appeared. Based on retention data, we aligned the conversion moment to the second adaptation cycle — when perceived value peaked at days 10–14.

🎯
Two-Tier Experience
Two parallel experiences designed in tandem: free users get core plan generation; paid users unlock advanced personalization, recovery-based adjustments, and priority modes — so the upgrade reads as a natural deepening, not a wall.
📈
Habit-First Flow
Paywall delayed until week 2, after users experience at least one adaptation cycle. Data showed peak value perception at days 10–14, not day 1.
🔄
Feedback Flywheel
Every completed workout feeds the engine: better data → better adaptations → higher retention → more data. A self-reinforcing loop.
🚀
Phased Rollout
Launched to 5% of new users, iterated on conversion data for two weeks, then scaled to 100% — de-risking paywall timing and adaptation quality.
04 Iterations

Wireframes for the two flows that mattered most: onboarding without form-fatigue, and the weekly adaptation moment. Iterating on these is where the “coach card” voice first emerged — before that, the surface read as a settings panel.

07 · Reflection

What I Learned

Trust is the product. The AI engine was sophisticated, but the design challenge was entirely about making users feel in control of it. The plain-language rationale was the highest-impact design decision — not the algorithm.

Timing is a design decision. Discovering that Sunday outperformed Monday for check-ins reminded me that when you interrupt a user matters as much as how.

0→1 requires protecting the vision. Stakeholders pushed to simplify the check-in to a single tap. I advocated for the conversational model based on research, and the retention data validated that choice.
